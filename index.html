Sisig

A sizzling pork delicacy with tang and spice.

Ingredients

  • 1kg pork maskara
  • 1 medium onion (finely chopped)
  • 3-5 cloves minced garlic
  • 2-3pcs red or green chili (chopped)
  • 2tbs clamansi or lemon juice
  • 2tbs soy sauce
  • 2tbs mayonaise (optional)
  • 1 egg (optional)
  • salt and pepper (to taste)
  • Coocking oil for frying and sautéing
  • 1tsp margarine or butter (optional)

Instructions

  1. Scrape off any remaining hair from the pork face, then wash thoroughly.
  2. Boil in a large pot with salt, peppercorns, bay leaves, and garlic until tender (about 1–2 hours).
  3. Remove from pot and let it cool.
  4. Grill or pan-fry the pork maskara until skin is slightly crispy and golden.
  5. In a pan, heat a little oil and sauté garlic and onion until fragrant.
  6. Add the chopped pork maskara and stir-fry for a few minutes. Season with soy sauce, salt, and pepper.
  7. Add calamansi juice and chili. If using, stir in a bit of mayonnaise for a creamy twist.
  8. Heat a sizzling plate, add a teaspoon of margarine or butter.
  9. Add the sisig mixture and crack an egg on top.
  10. Mix it all together while hot and Enjoy!

Tinola

Tinola is a Filipino chicken soup flavored with ginger, garlic or onion and fish sauce and traditionally includes green papaya.

Ingredients

  • 1 tbsp cooking oil
  • 1 small chopped onion
  • 2 cloves chopped garlic
  • Ginger, cut into strips
  • ½ kilo chicken, cut into 8 pieces
  • 4 cups water
  • 2 Knorr chicken cubes
  • 1 chayote or small green papaya, sliced
  • 2 stalks moringa leaves
  • 1 tbsp patis (fish sauce)
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

  1. Get a pot and bring it up to medium heat before pouring in the oil. Drop in the onions, garlic and ginger and sauté slowly for about 2 minutes or until you can smell the lovely aroma.
  2. It’s time to drop in the chicken pieces and stir until it turns white or light brown in color.
  3. Pour in the water and add your Knorr Chicken Broth cubes. Bring this to a simmer until chicken is tender and cooked through.
  4. You can now add your sayote or green papaya and cook until tender.
  5. Dahon ng sili is added at the last stage to ensure leaves (and nutrients) don’t dry up. Give this a minute then it is done. Malunggay is also another healthy alternative because it is packed with vitamins and minerals which are good for nursing moms and kids as well.
  6. Enjoy this with patis and calamansi on the side. See the faces of your whole family light up as you bring this to the table.

Adobo

The quintessential Filipino dish known for its savory and tangy flavor.

Ingredients

  • 1kg Chicken or Pork
  • ½ cup Soy sauce
  • ½ cup Vinegar
  • 1 cup Water
  • 5 cloved Garlic (crushed)
  • 3 pcs Bay leaves
  • 1 tsp whole Peppercorn
  • salt and sugar (to taste)
  • cooking oil

Instructions

  1. In a bowl or pot, combine the chicken or pork, soy sauce, vinegar, crushed garlic, bay leaves, and peppercorn. Let this marinate for at least 30 minutes to soak up the flavors.
  2. After marinating, place the pot on medium heat and bring everything to a boil. Do not stir right away — let the vinegar cook off a bit.
  3. Once it’s boiling, lower the heat and simmer for 30–40 minutes or until the meat is tender and fully cooked. Add water if the sauce reduces too much.
  4. Optional but tasty — remove the meat from the sauce and fry it in a bit of oil until lightly browned for a more savory touch.
  5. Return the fried meat back to the sauce and simmer for a few more minutes until everything is well-coated and flavorful.
  6. Serve hot with a big serving of rice and enjoy the rich, garlicky, savory taste of classic Filipino Adobo.

Pancit Bihon

Pancit is a general term in Filipino cuisine that refers to various traditional noodle dishes.

Ingredients

  • 250g pancit bihon
  • 200g chicken breast or thigh (sliced)
  • 1 cup shrimp or sliced pork ( optional)
  • 2-3 cloves minced garlic
  • 1 medium chopped onion
  • 1 medium carrot
  • 1 cup shredded cabbage
  • ½ cup green beans/baguio beans
  • 2tbs soy sauce
  • 2tbs oyster sauce (optional)
  • ½tsp ground pepper
  • 2-3 cups chicken broth or water
  • 2tbs cooking oil
  • calamnsi/lemon slices (for servings)

Instructions

  1. Soak the bihon noodles in warm water for 10–15 minutes or until slightly soft.
  2. Heat oil in a large pan or wok.
  3. Sauté garlic and onion until fragrant and soft.
  4. Add the chicken (and shrimp/pork if using). Stir-fry until cooked through. Season with soy sauce, oyster sauce, and pepper.
  5. Add carrots, green beans, and a bit of broth or water. Let it simmer for 2–3 minutes. Add cabbage last and cook just until wilted.
  6. Pour in the remaining broth and bring to a gentle simmer.
  7. Add the soaked bihon noodles and gently toss to mix with the sauce and veggies.
  8. Stir occasionally until the noodles absorb the liquid and are fully cooked (about 5–7 minutes).
  9. Adjust taste (add more soy sauce, salt, or pepper if needed).
  10. Serve hot with calamansi or lemon on the side.

Bistek Tagalog

Bistek (from Spanish: bistec, "beefsteak"), also known as bistek tagalog or karne frita, is a Filipino dish consisting of thinly-sliced beefsteak braised in soy sauce, calamansi juice, garlic, ground black pepper, and onions cut into rings.

Ingredients

  • ½ kg beef tenderloin/sirloin (thinly sliced)
  • ¼ cup soy sauce
  • ¼cup calamansi/lemon juice
  • 1tsp ground black pepper
  • 3-5 cloves minced garlic
  • 2 large onions (sliced into rings)
  • 2tbs cooking oil
  • salt (to taste)
  • ½ cup water (optional)

Instructions

  1. In a bowl, combine the sliced beef, soy sauce, calamansi juice, garlic, and black pepper.
  2. Mix well and marinate for at least 30 minutes to 1 hour (the longer, the better).
  3. In a pan, heat 1 tablespoon of oil over medium heat.
  4. Sauté the onion rings until soft and translucent (not mushy). Remove the onions and set aside.
  5. In the same pan, add another tablespoon of oil. Add the marinated beef (reserve the marinade) and sear until lightly browned.
  6. Pour in the remaining marinade and add water if desired for more sauce.
  7. Simmer for about 15–20 minutes, or until beef is tender and the sauce is reduced.
  8. Add the cooked onion rings back into the pan. Let it simmer for another 2–3 minutes. Adjust seasoning if needed.
  9. Serve hot with steamed white rice.

Kare-kare

A creamy peanut stew served with bagoong (fermented shrimp paste).

Ingredients

  • ½kg oxtail
  • 200g beef tripe
  • 1 small banana heart
  • 1 small eggplant (sliced)
  • 1 bunch string beans
  • 1 bundle pechay/bok choy
  • ½ cup peanut butter
  • ¼ cup toasted rice flour
  • 2tbs annatto seeds soaked in ¼ cup hot water for color
  • 1 onion (chopped)
  • 4 cloves minced garlic
  • salt and pepper (to taste)
  • 6-8 cups water or broth

Instructions

  1. Boil oxtail and tripe in water with a bit of salt until tender (about 2–3 hours). You can use a pressure cooker to speed up the process (~30–40 minutes).
  2. Soak annatto seeds in hot water for a few minutes. Mash and strain to extract the red color. Discard the seeds.
  3. In a large pan or pot, heat some oil. Sauté onion and garlic until soft and aromatic.
  4. Add the cooked meat and sauté for a few minutes.
  5. Pour in about 4–5 cups of broth from earlier. Stir in the peanut butter until fully blended.
  6. Add the annatto water and rice flour gradually to thicken the sauce. Simmer for 10–15 minutes, stirring occasionally.
  7. In a separate pot or in the same pan (if there’s room), blanch the vegetables (banana heart, eggplant, string beans, pechay) in boiling water or steam them until just tender.
  8. Add them on top of the stew or serve on the side.
  9. Serve hot with steamed rice and a side of bagoong (sautéed shrimp paste).

Sinigang

A sour tamarind-based soup with a mix of vegetables and meat.

Ingredients

  • 1kg pork belly or Ribs
  • 1 onion (quartered)
  • 2 tomatoes (quartered)
  • 1 liter water
  • 1 pack sinigang mix (tamarind flavor)
  • 1 radish (sliced)
  • 1 eggplant (sliced)
  • string beans
  • kangkong leaves
  • salt or fish sauce (to taste)

Instructions

  1. Start by boiling water in a large pot. Add the pork belly or ribs and simmer until scum forms on the surface. Skim this off to keep the broth clear.
  2. Add the onion and tomatoes and let it simmer with the meat until everything softens and the broth becomes flavorful.
  3. Pour in your sinigang mix and stir well. Let it dissolve completely to release that signature sourness.
  4. Add sliced radish, eggplant, and string beans into the pot. Let these cook until tender but not mushy.
  5. Add kangkong leaves (or other leafy greens like pechay) last. Simmer for just 1–2 minutes so they stay fresh and green.
  6. Serve hot with rice and enjoy the comforting, tangy soup that’s perfect for any weather.

Dinuguan

A rich and savory stew made from pork and pig’s blood.

Ingredients

  • 1lbs pork (cut into cubes)
  • 1 cup Vinegar
  • 2 pcs long green pepper
  • ½tbs brown sugar
  • 1 pc onion (chopped finely)
  • 1tbs minced garlic
  • 3tbs cooking oil
  • 1 cup Water
  • 10oz pork blood
  • 1 pc pork cube boullion
  • salt and pepper (to taste)

Instructions

  1. Heat oil in a pan. Sauté garlic and onion.
  2. Once the onion softens, add pork. Continue to sauté for 5 minutes or until light brown.
  3. Pour water. Let boil.
  4. Add pork cube. Cook in low heat until the meat tenderizes.
  5. Add pork blood. Stir. Cook for 8 to 10 minutes while stirring.
  6. Pour vinegar and add sugar and long green pepper. Cook for 12 to 15 minutes.
  7. Season with salt and pepper as needed. Serve hot with puto.

Kinilaw

A fresh and tangy raw fish salad with vinegar and spices.

Ingredients

  • 1pc ginger
  • ½ small red onion
  • 1 to 2 red Thai chiles
  • ½ cup fresh or bottled calamnsi juice
  • ¾ cup coconut or cane vinegar
  • 1lb sashimi-grade yellow fin tuna/spanish mackerel
  • ½tsp kosher salt
  • ¼tsp freshly ground black pepper

Instructions

  1. Prepare the aromatics, Prepare the following, adding them to a medium bowl as you complete them: Peel and very finely chop a 2-inch piece of ginger (about 2 tablespoons), Very thinly slice 1/2 small red onion (about 1/2 cup), Very thinly slice 1 to 2 Thai chiles into rounds (use 1 for less heat).
  2. Marinate the aromatics, Add 1/2 cup calamansi juice and 1/4 cup of the coconut vinegar and stir to combine, Refrigerate to allow the flavors to meld while you prepare the fish.
  3. Cube the fish, Cut 1 pound sashimi-grade fish (discard any skin) or shrimp into 1/2-inch cubes, Place in a medium, non-reactive bowl.
  4. “Wash” the fish in vinegar. Add the remaining 1/2 cup coconut vinegar and stir to combine, Let sit for 2 minutes, then stir again, Strain the fish through a fine-mesh strainer into the sink, gently pressing the fish with the back of a spoon.
  5. Combine the fish with the aromatics. Add the fish to the ginger mixture, season with 1/2 teaspoon kosher salt and 1/4 teaspoon black pepper, and toss until completely coated.
  6. “Cook” the fish in vinegar. Refrigerate until the fish is “cooked” to your desired doneness, 10 minutes to 1 hour. The fish will firm up as it sits. Taste and season with more salt and pepper as needed. Stir in 2 tablespoons coconut milk to soften the sharp vinegar flavors if desired.

Pinakbet

A vegetable medley flavored with fermented shrimp paste.

Ingredients

  • 1lbs lechon kawali (sliced)
  • 1pc knorr shrimp cube
  • string beans
  • ½pc kalabasa (cubed)
  • 12pcs okra
  • 1pc eggplant
  • 1pc ampalaya
  • 2pcs tomato (cubed)
  • 1pc chopped onion
  • 4 cloves crushed garlic
  • 2tsp bagoong alamang
  • 2&½ cups of water
  • 3tbs cooking oil
  • ¼tsp ground black pepper

Instructions

  1. Heat cooking oil in a pot. Saute onion and garlic. Add ginger and continue to cook until the onion softens.
  2. Add lechon kawali, Saute for 1 minute.
  3. Pour water. Let boil.
  4. Add Knorr Shrimp Cube, Cover the pot and cook in medium heat for 20 minutes.
  5. Add tomato and bagoong alamang, Stir, Cover the pot, Cook for 3 minutes.
  6. Put the kalabasa and kamote into the pot Cook for 7 minutes.
  7. Add sitaw, okra, ampalaya, and eggplant, Stir, Cover the pot and cook for 5 minutes.
  8. Season with ground black pepper and add remaining lechon kawali, Cook for minutes.
  9. Tranfer to a serving plate, Serve, Share and Enjoy!

Bulalo

A comforting beef marrow soup simmered to perfection.

Ingredients

  • 2lbs beef shank
  • ½ cabbage
  • 1 bundle bok choy
  • 2 cobs corn (cut into 3 parts)
  • 2tbs whole peppercorn
  • ½ cup green onions
  • 1 onion
  • 34oz water
  • fish sauce

Instructions

  1. In a big cooking pot, pour in water and bring to a boil.
  2. Put-in the beef shank followed by the onion and whole pepper corn then simmer for 1.5 hours (30 mins if using a pressure cooker) or until meat is tender.
  3. Add the corn and simmer for another 10 minutes.
  4. Add the fish sauce,cabbage, pechay, and green onion (onion leeks).
  5. Serve hot. Share and Enjoy!

Laing

A Bicolano dish made with dried taro leaves simmered in rich coconut milk, often with pork or shrimp and spiced with chili. It's creamy, spicy, and goes perfectly with rice.

Ingredients

  • 3 cups dried gabi (taro) leaves
  • 2 cups Coconut Milk
  • 2 cups Coconut Cream
  • 200g pork belly (sliced thin)
  • 1 small onion (chopped)
  • 3-4 cloved minced garlic
  • 1 thumed sized piece of ginger,(sliced thin)
  • 2-4pcs rwd chili
  • 2tbs bagoong alamang
  • salt and pepper (to taste)

Instructions

  1. In a pot, combine coconut cream, onion, garlic, ginger, and pork.
  2. Simmer over medium heat for 10–15 minutes until pork is partially cooked and the mixture is slightly thick.
  3. Gently lay the dried taro leaves on top of the simmering coconut mixture.
  4. Do not stir — stirring early may cause the taro to become itchy.
  5. Let it simmer uncovered on low heat until the taro leaves start to wilt and absorb the liquid (about 10–15 minutes).
  6. Once the leaves have softened and shrunk, pour in the coconut milk Add chili peppers and bagoong.
  7. Simmer gently for another 20–30 minutes, uncovered, until the oil starts to release and the leaves are very tender.
  8. Now you can stir gently to mix everything well.
  9. Serve hot with steamed rice.

Crispy Pata

Crispy pata is a popular Filipino dish consisting of deep-fried pork leg, typically the hock or trotters, known for its crispy skin and tender, juicy meat.

Ingredients

  • 1pc whole pig leg (cleaned)
  • 6pcs dried bay leaves
  • 2tbs whole peppercorn
  • 4pcs star anise (optional)
  • 6tsp salt
  • 2tsp ground black pepper
  • 2tsp garlic powder
  • 12 cups water
  • 6 cups cooking oil

Instructions

  1. Clean the pork leg thoroughly and scrape off any hair from the skin.
  2. In a large pot, combine water, pork leg, salt, garlic, peppercorns, and bay leaves. Add soy sauce or vinegar if desired.
  3. Bring to a boil, then simmer for 1.5 to 2 hours or until meat is tender but not falling apart.
  4. Remove the pork leg from the pot and drain well. Pat dry with paper towels.
  5. Heat enough oil in a deep fryer or large pot (enough to cover at least half of the pork leg).
  6. Once the oil is very hot (about 375°F or 190°C), carefully lower the pork leg using tongs or a slotted spoon.
  7. Fry for about 10–15 minutes or until the skin is golden brown and crispy.
  8. Slice into serving portions and Enjoy!

Atchara

Atchara, also spelled achara or atsara, is a Filipino pickled relish typically made from grated unripe papaya.

Ingredients

  • 3-4lbs green papaya
  • 2pcs carrot
  • 1pc sliced thinly onion
  • 10 cloves thinly sliced garlic
  • 2tbs whole peppercorn
  • 1pc red bell pepper
  • 1knob ginger
  • ¼ cup salt to dehydrated papaya
  • ½tsp salt for the brine or syrup
  • 2 cups white vinegar
  • 1&⅓ cups granulated sugar
  • ½ cup raisins

Instructions

  1. Peel, deseed, and grate the green papaya.
  2. Mix with 2 tablespoons of salt and let sit for 30 minutes to 1 hour to draw out moisture.
  3. Afterward, squeeze out the liquid using clean hands or a cheesecloth. Set aside.
  4. In a saucepan, combine vinegar, sugar, salt, peppercorns, and bay leaves.
  5. Bring to a boil, stir until sugar dissolves.
  6. Turn off heat and let cool slightly (it should still be warm when pouring into the jars).
  7. In a large bowl, mix the papaya with carrots, bell peppers, onions, garlic, ginger, and raisins.
  8. Pack this vegetable mixture tightly into sterilized glass jars.
  9. Pour the warm (not boiling hot) vinegar mixture into the jars until the vegetables are fully submerged.
  10. Tap the jars gently to remove air bubbles and seal tightly.
  11. Let the jars cool to room temperature, then refrigerate.
  12. Best enjoyed after 2–3 days of pickling. Can last for weeks if kept sealed and refrigerated.

Batangas Lomi

A rich, thick, and hearty Filipino noodle soup that’s perfect for a filling merienda or rainy-day meal.

Ingredients

  • 500g fresh lomi noodles
  • 250g pork kasim or belly (sliced thin)
  • 100g pork liver (optional)
  • 3-4pcs kikiam or fish balls
  • 1pc egg (beaten)
  • 4-5 cups water or pork broth
  • 1tbs soy sauce
  • 1tbs cornstarch + 3tbs water (slurry)
  • 4 cloves minced garlic
  • 1 small onion (chopped)
  • 2tbs cooking oil

Instructions

  1. In a pot, heat oil. Sauté garlic and onion until fragrant. Add the pork slices and cook until lightly browned. Add the liver and kikiam. Sauté for 2–3 minutes.
  2. Pour in the water or broth.
  3. Bring to a boil, then simmer for 10–15 minutes or until pork is tender.
  4. Add the fresh lomi noodles and cook for 5–7 minutes or until tender.
  5. Stir in the cornstarch slurry to thicken the soup.
  6. Lower the heat, then slowly pour the beaten egg while gently stirring to create egg ribbons.
  7. Adjust seasoning with salt, pepper, or more soy sauce.
  8. Ladle into bowls and top with chicharon, hard-boiled egg, fried garlic, and spring onions.
  9. Serve hot with calamansi, vinegar with chili, or toyo-mansi for dipping.
